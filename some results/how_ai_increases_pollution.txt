# Research Report: The Environmental Impact of Artificial Intelligence

## Executive Summary
The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence (AI) is driving a significant increase in energy consumption, leading to substantial environmental and public health concerns. AI’s environmental footprint is primarily defined by the electricity required for training and operating large-scale models, as well as the water usage necessary to cool the data centers housing these systems. While AI offers potential benefits for climate mitigation, its current growth trajectory is linked to increased greenhouse gas emissions, toxic air pollution, and resource depletion, often disproportionately affecting low-income communities.

## Key Findings
*   **Air Pollution and Public Health:** AI-related energy demand is increasing air pollution from power plants and backup diesel generators, with projections suggesting 1,300 premature deaths annually in the U.S. by 2030 [1].
*   **Energy Intensity:** Training large language models (LLMs) and performing "inference" (responding to queries) requires massive electricity consumption, often sourced from fossil-fuel-dependent grids [2, 3, 5].
*   **Water Depletion:** Data centers consume large volumes of freshwater for cooling, often in regions already facing water scarcity [3].
*   **Embodied Emissions:** Beyond operational energy, AI contributes to pollution through "embodied emissions" generated during the manufacturing of hardware (such as GPUs) and the construction of physical data center infrastructure [4].
*   **Reporting Gaps:** Tech industry sustainability reports often prioritize carbon emissions while frequently omitting data on non-carbon air pollutants and the localized health impacts of their operations [1].

## Detailed Analysis

### Air Pollution and Public Health Impacts
A study by UC Riverside and Caltech researchers highlights that the public health burden of AI is frequently overlooked. The demand for electricity to power data centers forces power plants and backup diesel generators to operate at higher capacities, releasing fine particulate matter and nitrogen oxides into the atmosphere [1]. These pollutants do not remain local; they drift across state and county lines, creating regional health costs estimated at $190 million to $260 million annually in the Northern Virginia area alone [1]. The study projects that by 2030, the public health burden associated with AI will rival that of all cars, buses, and trucks in California [1].

### Energy Consumption and Carbon Footprint
AI’s environmental impact is rooted in its heavy reliance on electricity. Training a single large model can consume over 1,200 MWh of energy [2]. While some sources note that AI currently accounts for a small fraction of global electricity consumption—estimated at approximately 0.04% [4]—the sector is the fastest-growing area for energy demand [1]. Carbon emissions are generated when this electricity is sourced from coal or natural gas plants [3]. There is a distinction between "embodied emissions," which occur during the construction of hardware and infrastructure, and "operational emissions," which occur during the active computation phase [4].

### Water Scarcity
Data centers require significant amounts of water to manage the heat generated by high-performance chips [3]. This process often diverts freshwater away from local communities and other essential uses. Bloomberg reports that over half of the data centers built since 2022 are located in regions where water demand already exceeds supply [3]. Furthermore, because the electricity used by these centers is often generated by power plants that also require water for cooling, the total water footprint of AI is compounded [3].

### Disproportionate Impact and Accountability
The environmental and health costs of AI are not distributed equally. Low-income communities are often situated in closer proximity to data centers and their associated power plants, bearing the brunt of the toxic air pollution [1]. Experts argue that tech companies should be required to adopt standardized reporting for air pollutants and provide compensation to communities impacted by the health burdens of data center operations [1]. Currently, tech companies often focus their sustainability reporting on carbon emissions and water usage, leaving other harmful air pollutants largely unaddressed [1].

## Conclusion
The growth of AI presents a complex environmental challenge. While the technology holds potential for aiding decarbonization efforts, its current infrastructure relies heavily on energy-intensive processes that contribute to climate change, water scarcity, and hazardous air pollution. As the sector continues to expand, addressing these impacts will require more transparent reporting, a shift toward cleaner energy sources, and a greater focus on the localized public health consequences of data center operations.
